📖 Introduction

Systemic sclerosis (SSc) is a chronic autoimmune disease with vasculopathy and progressive fibrosis. Renal involvement includes scleroderma renal crisis (SRC) or drug-induced nephropathy (e.g. penicillamine).

Nephrotic syndrome (NS): proteinuria >3.5 g/24h · hypoalbuminaemia <3.0 g/dL · generalised oedema.

📄 Abstract
A 37-year-old woman on chronic penicillamine presented with 2 weeks of progressive generalised oedema. Investigations revealed albumin 1.9 g/dL, proteinuria 466 mg/dL, and oliguria — confirming nephrotic syndrome. Serology and capillaroscopy established systemic sclerosis (SSc). Penicillamine withdrawal and supportive care led to significant improvement.

🎯 Learning Points
  1. Always take a full drug history in oedema — penicillamine is a reversible, underrecognised cause of NS.
  2. Penicillamine-MN vs SRC: same disease, different mechanism — ACEi lifesaving in SRC, unnecessary in drug-induced MN.
  3. Correct Ca²⁺ for albumin: Ca + 0.8 × (4.0 − Alb). Here: 8.68 mg/dL → avoid over-treating hypocalcaemia.
  4. "Undifferentiated CTD" needs reassessment — SSc may emerge via capillaroscopy and autoantibody profiling.
  5. Monitor penicillamine every 1–3 months (urinalysis, CBC, albumin). Proteinuria >1 g/day → immediate withdrawal.
  6. Early withdrawal is curative — prevents immunosuppression and CKD progression.
💡 Conclusion: Drug reconciliation and structured DMARD monitoring are essential. Maintain a low threshold for drug-induced glomerulopathy in SSc + oedema. Early withdrawal → full recovery.
